The drop is not general — it's math

Every subject slips. Math falls off a cliff.

If kids just liked school less as they got older, every subject would decline together. They don't. Between Grade 3 and Grade 6, "I like this subject" drops 4 points in writing, 10 in reading — and 19 in math.

Self-belief follows the same pattern: "I'm good at math" drops 64% → 50%, the steepest fall of any subject. At Grade 3, math is nearly the best-liked subject in school. By Grade 6 it is dead last.

And it isn't an artifact of one system: in Ontario's French-language schools — different assessments, different classrooms, higher achievement — liking math falls 79% → 61% over the same grades.

The part nobody expects

High school doesn't make it worse. It inherits the wreckage.

Ask Grade 9 students the same questions: 50% like math, 51% think they're good at it. Statistically indistinguishable from Grade 6. The collapse happens during the Junior years — and then it stops.

For everyone in this room, that's the argument. The intervention window isn't Grade 9 or Grade 12. It's us. It's now. It's Grades 4 to 6.

EQAO, 2024–25: Grade 9 Assessment of Mathematics student questionnaire.

The inquiry question

Why does math engagement collapse for some Junior students and not others — and which classroom factors actually move the needle?

Your turn — sort the evidence

Six suspected causes. Which does the research back?

For each factor, rate the evidence that it drives the Junior math engagement drop: big impact, medium, or little / none. Then check your sorting. Three of these hold up. Three mostly don't.

Students' belief about whether they're "a math person"
Big impact. Competence beliefs decline across Grades 1–12 and drag the valuing of math down with them; math is the steepest domain (Jacobs et al., 2002).
Math anxiety
Big impact. Takes root in upper elementary, climbs through high school, and selectively derails high-working-memory students (Hembree, 1990; Ramirez et al., 2016).
Grades and report cards
Little / none. Longitudinally, grades and intrinsic motivation are only weakly linked — effects small at best, and only in windows where grades carry real weight (Weidinger et al., 2017).
Their teacher's own feelings and beliefs about math
Big impact. Teacher math anxiety predicts student achievement over a single year; believing math ability is innate predicts lower motivation in low achievers (Beilock et al., 2010; Heyder et al., 2020).
Whether lessons match each student's learning style
Little / none. The style-matching hypothesis has repeatedly failed properly controlled tests. Widely believed; essentially unsupported (Pashler et al., 2008).
Amount of math homework assigned
Little / none. In elementary grades, homework volume shows little to no relationship with achievement, and none worth banking on for engagement (Cooper et al., 2006).

What the research says

The suspects, graded like we grade everything else

Each hypothesis gets an evidence rating on a scale this room already knows: Ontario achievement levels. Level 4 = strong, replicated, longitudinal evidence. Level 1 = plausible story, thin data.

1 · Competence beliefs fall — and drag value down

Evidence
Level 4

The workhorse finding of 40 years of expectancy-value research: kids' belief that they're good at math declines across Grades 1–12, and how much they value math follows it down. Part of this is kids becoming accurate — early-grade optimism fades as social comparison ramps up.

The danger isn't the accuracy. It's the interpretation: "I'm behind" hardening into "I'm not a math person."

Jacobs et al. (2002), 761 students, Grades 1–12; Wigfield & Eccles (2000).

2 · Math anxiety takes root in exactly this window

Evidence
Level 4

Math anxiety emerges in upper elementary and climbs through high school. And here's the twist: it hits hardest among students with the highest working-memory capacity — anxious high-ability kids abandon advanced strategies and underperform peers with less raw horsepower.

Anxiety doesn't just hurt your strugglers. It selectively kneecaps your strongest students.

Hembree (1990); Ma (1999); Ramirez et al. (2016).

3 · We transmit our own math attitudes

Evidence
Level 3

Teachers who believe math ability is innate have students — especially low achievers — with measurably lower intrinsic motivation. And in the classic Beilock study, elementary teachers' own math anxiety predicted lower math achievement in their students over a single school year.

Junior math is mostly taught by generalists, many of whom would place themselves in the 52% who don't like math. That's not a criticism. It's an inheritance — and it's the factor most under our control.

Heyder et al. (2020); Beilock et al. (2010, PNAS).

4 · Classrooms get more evaluative just as kids need autonomy

Evidence
Level 3

"Stage–environment fit": as students get older, classrooms tend to become more controlling and comparison-heavy — precisely when the developmental need for autonomy is rising. Where that mismatch was strongest, math expectancies and values fell fastest.

Caveat, in red pen: most of this evidence comes from the Grade 6→7 transition. Apply to Junior with care.

Eccles & Midgley (1989); Wigfield et al. (1991).

5 · The abstraction wall: fractions, ratios, multi-step

Evidence
Level 2

Grades 4–6 is where math stops being counting and starts being structure. Motivation research shows sharper declines when students hit cognitively demanding content — but this is the hypothesis every practitioner believes with the thinnest rigorous evidence behind it.

Honest label: plausible, under-tested. If your gut says "it's the fractions," the research neither confirms nor acquits.

Situational-motivation studies of demanding content; evidence base still thin.

The suspect that walks free

"Grades kill motivation."

This is the villain most of us would name first — and if the sorting exercise caught you, you're in good company. The longitudinal evidence says otherwise: across elementary school, trajectories of grades and intrinsic motivation are only weakly associated, and effects of one on the other are small at best.

The nuance worth keeping: grades appear to matter in the specific windows when they carry weight — like right before a school transition. Not harmless. But not the engine of the collapse either.

Why does this matter for us? Because every hour we spend re-litigating assessment is an hour not spent on the suspects with stronger evidence — the messages kids absorb about who math is for.

Weidinger et al. (2017), British Journal of Educational Psychology; latent growth models, German elementary students.

The most hopeful finding

The cliff is an average. Some kids never fall.

Growth-curve studies show the decline is not uniform: trajectories differ sharply across students. Some hold their engagement straight through. Which means the drop is not a law of child development — it's a variable. And variables have causes we can work on.

That's why the inquiry question asks about some students and not others. The interesting research question — and the actionable one — is what separates the fallers from the non-fallers in the same building, same curriculum, same year.

Weidinger et al. (2017); Garon-Carrier et al. (2016), 1,478 Canadian students, Grades 1–4.

So what do we do

What actually moves the needle

Two moves to invest in, matched to the strongest evidence:

  1. Keep autonomy in the room. Choice of method, choice of representation, open-middle tasks — small doses of control over how the math gets done, right when the developmental need for it is rising.
  2. Make feedback about the strategy, not the person. "That regrouping move worked — where else could it work?" beats "you're so smart" and demolishes "it's okay, math isn't for everyone." Behind is a position, not an identity.

Two moves to retire, because the evidence runs the other way: consolation talk that quietly lowers the bar, and shipping strugglers into a separate low group. Both feel like kindness. Both read to kids as a verdict.

And underneath all of it: audit our own math talk. If we wouldn't say "I'm just not a reading person" in front of students, the math version deserves the same discipline. Protect regular low-stakes math time — number talks, estimation, puzzles — where nothing counts and anxious kids can use their best strategies.

Reflection & next steps

Where this inquiry landed — and where it goes

I started this inquiry expecting to build a list of engagement strategies. The research redirected me twice: first, the drop is real but concentrated — in math, in Junior, in specific kids. Second, the causes I'd have bet on (grades, content difficulty) have weaker evidence than the one I'd least like to hear: the adults' relationship with math is part of the mechanism.

Next steps I'm committing to:

  1. Workshop session (today): groups by "suspect," each producing one classroom-sized move to try before the next PD day.
  2. Pull our own school's EQAO attitude data and put it beside the provincial numbers.
  3. A staff-room experiment: one month of tracking our own math self-talk around students.
  4. Revisit in spring: did anything move? What would we measure to know?
